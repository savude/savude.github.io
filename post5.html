Pearls of Wisdom: Avoid 'Easy' Things

Posted by Ellen Savude, on August 19, 2018

Recently I read this post Yossi Kreinin on the nature of work and why we ought to avoid ‘easy’ things. Here are some snippets from his orginal post:

Don’t Work on Easy Things

An easy thing is a heads they win, tails you lose situation. Failing at easy things is shameful; succeeding is unremarkable. Much better to work on hard things – heads you win, tails they lose. Failure is unfortunate but expected; success makes you a hero. The person working on the "hard" thing gets a lot of help and resources, making it easier to succeed – and easier to move on to the next "hard" thing. The guy doing the "easy" tasks gets no help, so it's harder to succeed. Quotation marks all over the place, because of course what counts is perception, not how hard or easy it really is.

My years-long preference for low-level programming results, in part, from its reputation of a very hard field, when in practice, it takes a little knowledge and a lot of discipline – but not any outstanding skills. Why then do many people fear low-level programming? Only because of how hard it bites you the first few times. People who felt that pain and recoiled respect those who've moved past it and reached productivity.

To a very large extent, your productivity is a result of what you choose to work on. Keep things perceived as easy out of that list. When you can't, postponing an "easy" thing can make it both "harder" and smaller.

Lesson: Stick to doing “harder” things because that way you’ll learn more, be able to collaborate and you’ll be more productive.
